01 · The market

Who the boutique owner is, and how she operates today

The target is the owner‑operator selling ready‑to‑wear, thrift ("okrika"), shoes, bags and accessories — from a shop, an Instagram page, or (usually) both at once.

🏬

The physical‑shop boutique

A rented stall in a plaza or market cluster — Balogun & Yaba (Lagos), Onitsha Main Market, Kano's Kantin Kwari, Aba. Buys stock in bulk on capital‑heavy restocking trips and resells at a markup.

📱

The Instagram / WhatsApp vendor

Posts photos, negotiates in DMs, closes on WhatsApp. 🟡 ~56% of MSMEs that sell online do so exclusively via social media (reported, directional).

♻️

The thrift / okrika seller

Graded second‑hand imports, from "bend‑down‑select" to curated first‑grade bales. Different unit economics: one‑of‑one items, condition matters, no clean size/colour matrix.

🔀

The hybrid (most of them)

Shop by day, Instagram by night, WhatsApp always. 🟡 For small fashion brands WhatsApp is the primary operating channel — orders, questions, payment, delivery — not just marketing.

🔵 Judgement: restocking is trip‑based and capital‑intensive (Turkey, China, Dubai, UK, plus local wholesale). The owner commits large sums on a plane‑ticket cadence, largely from gut feel — which makes "what should I re‑order?" a high‑value, under‑served question.
02 · The core pain

Running the business blind

This is the heart of the opportunity — and it's the best‑evidenced part of the research. The owner isn't asking for accounting. She's asking to stop losing money she can't see.

3hidden losses from managing by memory
Undetected revenue leakage, stock mismanagement, unrecorded debts. ✅ Verified (IRJMS 2026)
0defences against staff theft
Many Nigerian SMEs have "no preventive measures against employee theft." ✅ Verified (JFC 2018)
∞credit sales, untracked
Selling on credit is "a Nigerian small business reality," scattered across notebooks & chats. 🟡 Reported
Existing software is out of reach.
Accountants and accounting software are "prohibitive," pushing owners back to handwritten ledgers and spreadsheets. Foreign tools (QuickBooks/Excel) demand documentation discipline they lack. ✅ Verified
The barriers tell you how to build.
Top obstacles: inconsistent documentation, time constraints, small size, no money, difficulty using digital tools. ✅ Verified → every core action must take <5 seconds.
🔵 The synthesis: she is not asking for accounting — she's asking to stop losing money to stock, staff, and debtors, without becoming a bookkeeper. That's the product, and it's what we lead with.
03 · The buyer side

How Nigerians buy fashion — trust is the tax on every sale

Selling is the second priority, but buyer behaviour sharply constrains how a storefront or marketplace must work.

🔎

Discovery is social

~76M online shoppers, the majority buying predominantly on Facebook/Instagram rather than dedicated e‑commerce sites. A storefront not fed by social is a shop with no street.

🟡 Reported
🤝

Trust is the binding constraint

The recurring fear is "what I ordered vs what I got," and outright scams. Buyers de‑risk by paying on delivery.

🟡 Reported
📦

Pay‑on‑delivery verifies authenticity

POD persists specifically so buyers can confirm the product before paying. 🔵 A marketplace must engineer trust — verified sellers, ratings, pay‑to‑release — or buyers won't prepay strangers.

🟡 Reported
💬

Chat‑first — but mind the numbers

People negotiate in chat (well‑attested). But the viral "67% of purchases begin with a chat" stat is ❌ refuted (0‑3), and "informal vendors outcompete funded platforms in fashion" is ❌ refuted (1‑2). Use the behaviour; drop the stats.

❌ Refuted in verification
Market size, quoted carefully: Nigeria's social‑commerce market ≈ US$2.04B in 2025 → US$3.96B by 2030 (~14% CAGR) 🟡. Formal e‑commerce figures ($7.04B) surfaced but went unverified (agent hit a usage limit) — don't lead with them.
04 · Payments

Transfer‑first, cash‑real, card‑last

The winning payment UX is the one buyers already trust — not the one that maximises card fees.

🏦

Bank transfer dominates

48% of owners restock via transfer. The instant‑transfer rail (NIP) is the real payment method; cards are secondary. ✅

💵

Cash is still everywhere

Especially in‑shop and on pay‑on‑delivery. The app must log cash sales as fast as digital ones.

🧾

Proof‑of‑payment + reconciliation

Match Bumpa's stack (USSD, transfer, card, QR, "Proof of Payment," WhatsApp notifications) ✅ — then win on auto‑matching a transfer to an invoice.

🔵 Design rule: payment "collection" = generate a link/USSD/QR → confirm proof → auto‑reconcile against the sale. Not a wallet, not a POS‑terminal fleet, not lending — at least not to start. (That path killed Kippa's funded bet — see below.)

Kippa — the cautionary tale

The most valuable asset in this report: specific, verified, recent failure lessons.

💥

Price sensitivity is the master constraint

Kippa raised a fee from ₦25→₦35; backlash and churn threats forced a full revert to ₦25. We can't assume we can quietly raise prices later.

✅ Verified (TechCabal)
🔌

Don't monetise with FX‑exposed hardware

Exited agency banking Oct 2023 despite ~3 years of runway — a unit‑economics decision. 🟡 Naira devaluation made POS terminals prohibitive.

✅ / 🟡
🆓

A free base is not a business

500k merchants used Kippa's free bookkeeping — that base did not convert into a durable payments business. Design the paid value in from the start.

✅ Verified
🕳️

Abandonment poisons the whole category

When the app went dark (Jan 2024), merchants lost access to their own records. 🔵 Reliability + data portability are a moral and competitive necessity.

🟡 Reported
The real competitor isn't Bumpa or Kippa — it's WhatsApp + a notebook (free). That's the owner's status quo, and we win by beating it on speed and trust, not by out‑featuring a funded startup.
06 · Pricing tolerance

What a boutique will actually pay

Triangulated from three verified facts. This is the most business‑critical constraint in the report.

Informal owners earning <₦20,000/day ✅44%
Owners who restock by bank transfer ✅48%
Bumpa's price floor (₦5,000/mo) — our ceiling to undercut ✅₦5k
The price rise Kippa's users revolted against ✅+₦10
🔵 Pricing thesis: a genuinely useful free tier is near‑mandatory (the notebook is free); the paid anchor sits at ₦3,000–₦5,000/mo, justified by money recovered (debt collected, shrinkage caught), not features; billing is quarterly/annual to match the market and smooth cash‑flow; any transaction fee is near‑invisible and only on online storefront payments — never a tax on core takings.

08 · The wedge

Win the back office for fashion. Then selling becomes a toggle, not a separate product.

Land free, beating the notebook on sale/stock/debtor logging. Expand to storefront + payments once she trusts us with her money. Moat from vertical depth, the switching cost of her living data, and a marketplace fed by our own supply.

"Boutika is the operating system for your boutique. Know your stock, your money, and who owes you — then sell everywhere from the same place."
