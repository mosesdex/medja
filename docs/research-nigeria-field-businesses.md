# Nigeria's Field-Service Businesses: 30 Fields, the Platform Graveyard, and Three Niche App Opportunities

**Date:** July 2026
**Method:** Multi-agent deep research — 110 research agents across 6 search angles, 27 primary/secondary sources fetched, 134 claims extracted, top 25 adversarially verified with 3-vote panels (21 confirmed, 4 refuted), plus three targeted follow-up investigations (~110 additional web searches) covering per-field data, platform post-mortems, and monetization benchmarks.
**Context:** Compares adjacent niches against Mesjia (SaaS for Nigerian cleaning companies).

---

## Part 1 — The macro picture (verified claims)

Every claim below survived 3-vote adversarial verification.

1. **The market base is enormous but almost entirely micro-scale.** ~39.65M MSMEs as of Dec 2020, 96.9% micro; MSMEs are ~96–97% of all businesses, ~46.3% of GDP, ~84–88% of employment. Stable across every survey wave since 2010. *(NBS/SMEDAN 2021 survey; PwC MSME Survey 2024 — 3-0, six merged claims)*
2. **The trades are overwhelmingly informal.** ~90% of new jobs are informal; ILO/NBS put informal employment at ~92% of total. Any app targeting cleaners, plumbers, tailors or mechanics targets informal operators, not registered firms. *(Jobberman 2024; ILO Nov 2023 — 3-0)*
3. **Naira monetization is hard-capped by informal incomes.** 44% of informal business owners earn under ₦20,000/day revenue (70% under ₦50,000/day); most informal workers earn ₦30,000–60,000/month. Pricing must target business owners (companies) or use transaction models — not individual workers. *(Moniepoint Informal Economy Report 2025; Jobberman 2024 — 3-0 / 2-1)*
4. **Cash still dominates customer-facing payments** (~51% cash / 39% transfers / 9% cards; only 1 in 4 informal businesses earn 10%+ digitally). An app must record and reconcile cash jobs, not assume digital rails. *(Moniepoint 2025; CBN — 3-0)*
5. **Tools/equipment financing is a validated wedge.** ~₦13T unmet MSME credit demand; typical loan sought is under $2,000 — the ticket size of generators, sewing machines, cleaning equipment, and solar kits. Only ~2% of Nigeria's ~106M adults have bank credit access. *(IFC via PwC 2024 — 2-1)*
6. **Top SME pain points:** obtaining finance (22–35%), finding customers (16%), infrastructure (electricity = 21% of operating costs). *(PwC MSME surveys — 3-0)*
7. **WhatsApp is the de facto business platform.** 58% of self-employed Nigerian women and 65% of men use WhatsApp for livelihood activities; trust within existing social networks (family, church, neighbours) is the single most consistent requirement for transacting. This favours WhatsApp-native, referral/trust-centric products over anonymous marketplaces. *(Caribou/Turn.io, Ipsos n=7,000 incl. 2,000 Nigeria, June 2025 — 3-0)*
8. **SweepSouth Nigeria is the canonical marketplace failure.** Launched June 2022, announced shutdown Nov 2022 (~5 months). Cited unit economics, naira devaluation; ex-employees described an under-resourced, "half-hearted" expansion. Partly execution failure — leaving room for differently-shaped models. *(TechCabal Jan 2023 — 3-0, three merged claims)*
9. **Eden Life proves the premium consumer segment is tiny.** ~600 subscribers in 2021 at ~₦42,000/month (40% above then-minimum wage); pivoted to e-commerce Nov 2023, paused its entire consumer business Feb 2026 for B2B corporate catering. The affluent-consumer segment is too small and emigration-exposed. *(TechCabal, TechCrunch — 3-0, four merged claims)*

**Refuted in verification (do not reuse):** "95.3% of micro enterprises unregistered with CAC" (1-2), "88% smartphone / 5% digital payments adoption" (0-3), "informal sector >50% of GDP" (1-2), "1.3M registered POS agents" as stated (0-3 — better estimate is 1.5–2M terminals/agents from Technext/Intelpoint).

## Part 3 — The platform graveyard (what died, what survived, and why)

### Dead or retreated
| Platform | Model | Fate | Stated reason |
|----------|-------|------|---------------|
| **VConnect** | Directory → deals → e-commerce → services marketplace | Deadpooled (~2018–20); founder moved to OmniRetail | Whiplash pivots, no durable monetization of directory traffic |
| **SweepSouth NG** | On-demand cleaning marketplace | Dead in 5 months (Nov 2022) | Unit economics, devaluation + half-hearted ops (insiders) |
| **Eden Life** | Premium home-services subscription | Consumer business paused Feb 2026 | Tiny affluent segment, FX/inflation, **worker disintermediation** |
| **Kippa** | Free bookkeeping → agency banking | KippaPay shut Oct 2023; founders exited, site down Aug 2025 | Devaluation destroyed thin payment margins; 500K free users never monetized |
| **Jumia verticals** | Easy Taxi, Jumia Food etc. | Serial shutdowns (Food: Dec 2023, "never profitable") | Consumer unit economics |
| **FindWorka** | Artisan marketplace | Pivoted away from artisans within year 1 | "Challenges managing the artisan segment" |
| **Mocality, DealDey, Tress** | Directory / deals / beauty community | All dead | Pure directory/lead-gen and content-only models fail |

### Alive (and the shape they share)
| Platform | Model | Traction | Why it survives |
|----------|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **wrkman** | Vetted artisan marketplace, Lagos/Abuja/PH/Ibadan | 28,000 artisans, 100k+ jobs; bootstrapped | Escrow ("Secure Funds"), 30-day guarantee, cashback against off-platform leakage |
| **LaborHack** | Certified artisans for homes + construction, + Kenya | 4,500+ artisans, $320K (Techstars) | Vetting-first; certifies against UK ECITB standard (no credible local benchmark exists); free-redo guarantee |
| **Fixit45** | Auto workshops + parts | 300+ workshops, 9 cities, $1.9M | B2B-ish network, parts revenue |
| **Mecho Autotech** | Vehicle repair → parts/data | 35 states | Pivoted from service marketplace to parts supply |
| **Fitted / Stylebitt** | Tailoring SaaS + marketplace | 7,000 tailors / 19k signups | Lean, tools-first; Stylebitt's 26% activation shows WTP limits |
| **Bumpa** | Merchant social-commerce SaaS | 100k+ businesses, $4M seed | WhatsApp/IG-native; quarterly+ billing only |
| **Oze** | SME bookkeeping → lending software for banks | 150k users, 8 countries | **SMEs wouldn't pay; banks pay for the data** |
| **Fichaya** | Curated cleaning + academy | ~$12K/mo, 60+ homes, 270+ cleaners trained | Small, managed, trust-first — lifestyle scale |

### The five failure patterns (all source-stated)
1. **Devaluation kills thin margins** — every FX-exposed, low-take-rate model died (Kippa, SweepSouth, Eden Life, Jumia Food).
2. **Disintermediation** — after job #1, customer and worker cut the platform out. Survivors fight it with escrow, guarantees, cashback (wrkman) or recurring B2B contracts.
3. **Vetting cost of informal labour** broke FindWorka; survivors make vetting the *product* (LaborHack).
4. **Low willingness to pay** — free-tool-then-monetize failed at 500K users (Kippa); subscription-only is thin (Stylebitt 26% active). Survivors layer payments take-rate and/or embedded credit on a modest SaaS anchor.
5. **Consumer marketplaces die; B2B tooling survives.** The VC-funded consumer plays retreated; the bootstrapped, asset-light, B2B-pivoted players are the ones still standing. **This is direct validation of the Mesjia shape.**

## Part 4 — Proven monetization benchmarks (naira)

- **SaaS price points that hold:** entry **₦3,200–5,000/month** (Stylebitt Professional ₦3,200, Oze Silver ₦3,650, Bumpa Starter ₦5,000); mid **₦9,200–15,400/month** (Oze Gold, Bumpa Pro, Stylebitt Business); team/premium **₦22,900–45,800/month**. Below ₦1,000/month signals hobby-grade. Most SMEs buy the middle tier.
- **Retention mechanics:** quarterly/annual-only billing (Bumpa), free months on longer terms (Oze), free tier as funnel. USD pricing causes friction (Stylebitt user complaints).
- **WhatsApp Business API (July 2025 pricing):** utility/auth templates ≈ **$0.0074 (~₦11)** per message; all replies within the 24h service window **free**; click-to-WhatsApp ads open a 72h free window. A WhatsApp-native booking/reminder workflow costs near zero.
- **Embedded finance is the proven depth layer:** Moniepoint working capital at ~2–3%/month auto-deducted from transaction flows; Oze lends via bank partners off bookkeeping data; M-KOPA pay-as-you-go device ladders; Klump BNPL charges merchants 5%/transaction. Carbon (₦100K–9.5M), FairMoney (to ₦5M), Renmoney, Lendha cover the artisan ticket sizes.
- **Moniepoint POS "financing" reality check:** ₦21,500 upfront + ₦80,000/day minimum volume or repossession — hardware is leased against transaction volume, a model a vertical app can replicate for equipment.

## Part 5 — Three niche app opportunities (deep evaluation)

Selection criteria derived from the verified evidence: (a) genuine whitespace, (b) a payer who is a *business*, not an informal worker (finding #3), (c) structural resistance to disintermediation (failure pattern #2), (d) an association channel for cheap aggregation, (e) WhatsApp-native + Android-first fit, (f) an embedded-finance depth layer (finding #5).

### Opportunity 1 — Construction-trades workforce platform ("LinkedIn + payroll for building artisans")

**The niche:** bricklayers, carpenters, welders, tilers, electricians, plumbers working construction sites — aggregated as *crews* supplied to developers and contractors.

- **Gap size:** the best-documented deficit on the list. Nigeria needs ~1.5M artisans/yr, runs at <30% of required skilled manpower, and **loses $4.5B/yr to imported artisans** from Togo/Benin/Ghana. Lagos alone has 48,000 live projects. Contractors already pay premium rates to foreigners for *reliability of character and competence* — i.e., they pay for verification, which is exactly what software can productize.
- **Why incumbents are lacking:** LaborHack is the only meaningful player ($320K raised — subscale vs. the problem) and had to certify against a *UK* standard because no credible Nigerian benchmark exists. The generalist apps (wrkman etc.) serve households, not sites. NIOB and the new Sector Skills Council (target: 1M trained artisans in 5 years) need a digital registry — a partnership opening.
- **Model:** B2B. Contractors/developers pay — not artisans (respects the ₦20k/day income cap). Revenue: placement/day-rate take on crew supply, verified-artisan registry subscriptions for contractors (₦25k–50k/month fits the proven team tier), payroll/attendance SaaS per site. Depth layer: tools financing against verified work history (validated <$2,000 ticket), wage advances via lender partners.
- **Disintermediation resistance:** medium-high — the platform holds the *certification record, attendance log, and payroll rail*; a contractor poaching one bricklayer doesn't reproduce the vetted-crew pipeline for the next site.
- **WhatsApp/Android fit:** strong — foremen coordinate crews on WhatsApp today; job dispatch and attendance check-in via WhatsApp utility messages (~₦11 each) is cheap.
- **Risks:** operationally heavy (real-world vetting), construction payment cycles are slow, and quality failures on-site carry liability. This is the biggest prize but the heaviest lift.

### Opportunity 2 — Generator + AC/refrigeration maintenance OS ("power & cooling techs for SMEs")

**The niche:** the technicians who keep Nigeria's ~22M generators and its AC/cold-chain equipment running — sold as recurring preventive-maintenance contracts to the SMEs that depend on that equipment (shops, pharmacies, restaurants, hotels, estates, bank branches).

- **Gap size:** the deepest pure whitespace found. 22M generators ≈ 8× grid capacity, $22B/yr spent fuelling them, thousands of neighbourhood repair artisans — and **zero platforms**. AC side: only ~11,000 formally trained techs; NARAP/AHVACRPN associations exist but no digital layer. The #1 customer pain is part-swapping scams and opportunistic pricing — a trust problem, the verified core requirement (finding #7).
- **Why nobody serves it:** repair looks like a one-off marketplace (disintermediation trap) — but *maintenance* is recurring. The wedge is the service contract: quarterly servicing schedules, digital service-history log per machine, genuine-parts sourcing, WhatsApp reminders. The value accrues in the equipment's maintenance record, which lives on the platform — the same reason Jobber-style field-service SaaS works elsewhere and the exact Mesjia mechanic applied to a bigger installed base.
- **Model:** two-sided B2B: tech workshops pay ₦3,000–5,000/month solo / ₦10,000–15,000 team tier for scheduling + records + invoicing (proven cluster); SME customers optionally pay for maintenance plans, platform takes a cut on contract collections. Depth: parts marketplace margin (Mecho's pivot validates parts as the money layer in repair verticals), tool/gauge financing, and solar-transition upsell — the same techs are retraining into solar O&M, a 250,000-job wave.
- **Disintermediation resistance:** high — recurring contracts + service history + parts supply.
- **WhatsApp/Android fit:** excellent — reminder-driven ("your generator is due for servicing") utility messages at ₦11 are the product's heartbeat.
- **Risks:** two-sided cold start (mitigate by starting single-player: tools for the tech's existing customer book, like Mesjia); generator installed base shrinks over a 10-year solar horizon (mitigated by the same techs owning the solar/inverter maintenance market that replaces it).

### Opportunity 3 — Salon & barbershop booking + deposits ("Fresha for Nigeria")

**The niche:** hairdressers, barbers, and (adjacent) makeup artists and nail techs — fixed-location, appointment-driven beauty businesses.

- **Gap size:** $1.16B hair-care market heading to ~$1.96B by 2028, inside a $7.8B beauty market; NASHCO has organized the trade since 1979 (and issues local operating permits — a ready-made vetting + distribution channel). Booking is near-whitespace: Hairxify is tiny, Fresha/Booksy are barely localized, and Tress (content-only) died proving community without booking/payments fails.
- **Why incumbents are lacking:** global booking apps assume card-on-file for no-show protection — Nigeria runs on bank transfer (finding #4). The local killer feature is **transfer-based booking deposits**: the no-show/haggling pain both sides report, solved with a ₦2,000–5,000 deposit via payment link inside WhatsApp/Instagram DMs, where these businesses already sell (Bumpa proved ~40% of merchant orders originate there).
- **Model:** free booking page + WhatsApp bot as funnel; monetize a take on deposits/payments (devaluation-resistant, per failure pattern #1) plus ₦5,000/month Pro tier (client records, formulas, staff rota, chair-rental splits). Depth: dryer/clipper/chair equipment financing; product (extensions, dyes) supply margin later.
- **Disintermediation resistance:** medium — but the deposit *is* the retention mechanic: the shop wants deposits enforced, and the deposit rail only exists inside the platform. Recurring visit cadence (2–6 weeks) compounds the client-book lock-in.
- **WhatsApp/Android fit:** the strongest of the three — booking, reminders, and deposit links are all chat-native; solo-operator UX must be Android-first.
- **Risks:** lowest ceiling of the three (per-shop revenue is modest); crowded *globally* so a foreign player could localize; solo stylists' willingness to pay is thin — the deposit take-rate, not the subscription, must carry the model.

### Head-to-head

| Criterion | 1. Construction crews | 2. Generator/AC maintenance | 3. Salon booking |
|---|---|---|---|
| Documented gap | ★★★★★ ($4.5B leakage) | ★★★★★ (22M units, zero platforms) | ★★★☆☆ |
| Payer quality | Contractors (strong) | SMEs + workshops (strong) | Shops (modest) |
| Disintermediation resistance | Medium-high | **High** | Medium |
| Ops weight | **Heavy** | Medium | Light |
| Association channel | NIOB, Skills Council | NARAP, AHVACRPN | NASHCO |
| Embedded-finance depth | Tools + wage advance | Parts + tools + solar | Equipment + products |
| Fit with Mesjia playbook | Adjacent skills | **Same mechanic, bigger base** | Different (consumer-adjacent) |

### What this means for Mesjia

The verified evidence *validates the Mesjia thesis shape*: consumer marketplaces and premium subscriptions died (SweepSouth, Eden Life); low-priced B2B tooling for the service businesses themselves, WhatsApp-native, cash-aware, with embedded finance as the depth layer, is the surviving pattern. Cleaning is also *whitespace again* — both funded entrants retreated for reasons that were partly execution (SweepSouth) and partly wrong model (Eden Life's premium consumer subscription), not absent demand (>$200M/yr, growing 10–15%).

The sharpest strategic read: **Opportunity 2 is Mesjia's own mechanic pointed at a larger installed base**, and the two can share a codebase — job scheduling, service history, WhatsApp reminders, cash reconciliation, team payroll are identical modules. Cleaning companies and generator/AC technicians are both "field teams doing recurring jobs at customer premises." Rather than abandoning the cleaning niche, the research suggests building Mesjia's core as *field-service infrastructure* that can open a second vertical once the first has paying tenants.

## Part 6 — Caveats and open questions

- Field-by-field figures vary widely in quality: private security (828,502 — official NBS), POS (1.5–2M) and generators (22M) are strong; events ($20B) and tutoring ($250–400M) are looser industry estimates. The "$718B artisan industry" figure circulating in some reports is implausible (exceeds GDP) and was excluded.
- MSME counts are Dec 2020 vintage (most recent completed national survey). The ₦13T credit gap's $32.2B conversion uses pre-devaluation FX (roughly $8–9B at 2025–26 rates). Jobberman's ₦30k–60k earnings band is 2024-nominal and eroding at 25–34% inflation — which *strengthens* the affordability constraint.
- Moniepoint and Jobberman publish research with commercial motives; their claims used here were adversarially verified and, in Moniepoint's case, the cash-preference finding cuts against its own interests.
- Could not find reliable information on: Errandlads, iCraftBoss, HelloTailor (Nigeria), Justclean (Nigeria), Handyfixng (only a rebranded X handle survives).
- Unverified next questions: actual retention curves for wrkman/LaborHack; whether NARAP/AHVACRPN or NIOB would formally partner on a registry; real contract values for SME generator maintenance plans.
